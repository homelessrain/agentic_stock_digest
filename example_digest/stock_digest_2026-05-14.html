SECTION 3: THE NEXT TECHNOLOGICAL FRONTIER

The "Inference Inflection" and Edge AI Revolution

The semiconductor industry is undergoing a structural shift from its current phase (centralized GPU training in hyperscale data centers) toward three interrelated frontiers:

1. Inference Over Training — The "Second Wave"

By early 2026, over 55% of AI-optimized infrastructure spending is now dedicated to running models in production rather than training them. This transition has exposed a critical flaw in earlier data center architectures: while GPUs have become exponentially faster, the pipes connecting them have struggled to keep pace.

The smartest investors understand that the spending mix within AI infrastructure budgets is beginning to shift. While training remains Nvidia's domain, inference demands more power-efficient silicon that can be deployed at huge scale for a lower cost.

2. Edge AI — Moving the Brain to the Device

Perhaps the most significant shift in 2026 is the migration of AI inference from the cloud to the "edge." The market for on-device AI—spanning AI-enabled PCs, smartphones, and industrial IoT—is growing at a CAGR of over 26%. The goal is to run complex models locally on Neural Processing Units (NPUs) rather than sending every query to a distant server.

Every buildout has a bottleneck that eventually becomes unbearable. For the first decade of modern AI, that bottleneck was compute. Now we have a new one: latency. A self-driving car cannot wait 200 milliseconds for a data center in Virginia to decide whether the shape ahead is a pedestrian or a mailbox. A surgical robot cannot afford to buffer. The solution is simple: move the brain to the machine.

3. Agentic AI and Physical AI — The 2027+ Horizon

Looking ahead to the remainder of 2026 and into 2027, the industry is preparing for the rise of "Agentic AI"—systems that don't just answer questions but perform complex, multi-step tasks autonomously. These agents require even lower latency and more frequent access to vast "data lakes" than current chat-based models.

NVIDIA itself is expanding into this frontier, announcing it is expanding its open model families to power the next wave of agentic, physical and healthcare AI, introducing new models that enable developers and scientists to build intelligent systems that can reason and act across digital and real-world environments.

Business Context for This Shift

The key insight is that the current semiconductor rally is concentrated in data-center GPU/memory plays (NVIDIA, AMD, Micron, Broadcom). But the next wave of value creation will flow to:

  • Edge AI chip designers serving automotive ADAS, robotics, and industrial IoT
  • IP licensors whose neural processing unit designs get embedded in billions of devices
  • Low-power FPGA/programmable logic companies enabling AI customization at the edge
  • Custom silicon and connectivity companies solving the interconnect bottleneck

AI chips are now 0.2% of all chips manufactured, but they account for roughly 50% of total industry revenue. The enterprise-wide AI integration is not yet in place. Only 44% of organizations have fully embedded AI across multiple functions, while the remaining 56% are still operating in pilots, limited deployments, or early exploration.

The buying conversation is already shifting from peak compute performance to FLOPS-per-watt, latency-per-query, and cost-per-inference.

Future Catalyst: 2026 is expected to be the "year of Nexus" — Lattice's AI-optimized FPGA platform — with new design wins ramping in AI servers, automotive, and robotics. The mid-range Avant platform expands their addressable market significantly.

Estimated Price Recognition Timeline: Q3–Q4 2026, as inventory normalization completes and Lattice posts accelerating revenue growth that validates the AI FPGA thesis. Analyst target: ~$170 by end of 2028.
